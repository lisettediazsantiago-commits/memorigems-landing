Read a Caregiver Story

Short, true-to-life stories from people walking this path. Tap one to read.

The Tuesday I stopped apologizing
Renata, caring for her mother

For a year I said sorry constantly. Sorry I was late. Sorry I forgot. Sorry I snapped when she asked the same question for the ninth time before lunch.

One Tuesday a nurse stopped me mid-apology and said, “You’re doing the work of three people. What if you spoke to yourself like you speak to her?” I cried in the parking lot. Then I tried it. When I forgot something, I said, “That’s okay, you’re tired.” Out loud, like I would to Mom.

Nothing about the caregiving got easier. But I got kinder to the person doing it. That turned out to matter more than I expected.

What my father still knows
David, son and care partner

He doesn’t always know my name now. For a long time that felt like losing him twice.

Then I noticed the things that stayed. He still reaches for my mother’s hand. He still hums when the radio plays the old songs. He still says “thank you” to the woman who brings his tea, every single time, like it’s the first kindness anyone’s shown him.

What memory takes, it doesn’t take all at once. The man who taught me to be gentle is still gentle. I’ve stopped grieving who he forgot and started noticing who he still is.

Permission to rest
Aisha, caring for her husband

I thought love meant being there every minute. So I stopped sleeping, stopped seeing friends, stopped being a person who existed outside his needs.

My sister came for a weekend and made me leave the house. Two hours. I sat in a cafe and didn’t know what to do with my hands. When I came back, he was fine — better, even, because I came back lighter.

Rest isn’t abandoning him. An empty cup pours nothing. I take the two hours now. I’ve learned that staying whole is part of staying.
